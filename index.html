Natural Language Processing to Categorize Misinformation

A tool to help track mis/disinformation and provide rapid response

Project Information

In recent years there has been an abundance of mis/disinformation around US elections. This has caused a number of issues from fake news to voter fraud and the capitol insurrection in 2021.

In an effort to understand how mis/disinformation starts and spreads, we’re working with the University of Washington’s Center for an Informed Public to improve the research process of qualitative researchers by creating a Python Jupyter Notebook that allows researchers to gain insight from datasets relevant to the US election.

With our project, we hope to track mis/disinformation and provide rapid response ahead of the 2022 midterm elections.

Sponsors

This project is sponsored by the University of Washington’s Center for an Informed Public (UW CIP). Our Solution

We developed a comprehensive Python Jupyter Notebook that includes features to assist qualitative researchers in their research process. These features are sentiment analysis, search, standardize timezones, popular tweets, and filters (retweets, date, and time).

Sentiment Analysis

Find positive, negative and neutral tweets related to keyword/phrase/hashtag

Search

Find tweets related to a term and/or exclude term(s) using vectorization

Standardize Timezones

Convert timezones in the dataset (e.g., GMT to PST)

Filter (Date & Time)

Filter to find tweets by a specific date and time

Filter (Retweets)

Filter to find if a tweet was retweeted or not

Popular Tweets

Count the number of times a tweet was retweeted

With these tools, you can perform queries like:

  • Check tweets that were posted on January 6th, 2021 during the time of the Capital Insurrection in Georgia’s timezone
  • Look up which tweets similar to ‘fraud’ have been retweeted the most
  • Look at tweets with positive sentiment related to “President
  • Trump”Filter for all tweets mentioning “#stopthesteal” from election week
